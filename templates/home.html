What is Epilepsy?

A disorder in which nerve cell activity in the brain is disturbed, causing seizures. Epilepsy may occur as a result of a genetic disorder or an acquired brain injury, such as a trauma or stroke. During a seizure, a person experiences abnormal behaviour, symptoms and sensations, sometimes including loss of consciousness. Epilepsy is usually treated by medication and in some cases by surgery, devices or dietary changes.

Epilepsy is the second most common brain disorder after migraine, affecting 1% of world’s population Seizure symptoms can vary widely-some simply stare blankly for a few seconds, while others repeatedly twitch their arms or legs. Currently, Electroencephalogram (EEG) is used in clinical studies to decode the electrical activity of the brain.

Problems:
1. Visual inspection of EEG for seizure detection by expert neurologists is a time-consuming and laborious process,
2. The diagnosis may not be accurate because of the massive amounts of EEG data and the discrepant clinical judgment standards of different neurologists.
Hence, the automatic identification of epilepsy has become an important issue.
